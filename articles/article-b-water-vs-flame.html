Water Cremation vs. Flame Cremation: Which Is Right for Your Soul Dog?

Updated for 2026  ·  Target: Decision-makers in active grief

Both options are legitimate. Neither is wrong. But they're not the same, and understanding the actual differences will help you choose from a place of clarity rather than default.

There's a decision most of us don't think about until we have to make it, and then suddenly we have to make it fast, while we're not okay. If you're doing your research now, while you have time and some emotional breathing room, I want to give you a real comparison, not a sales pitch for either option.

What Each Process Is, Without the Clinical Language

Flame cremation, which most of us grew up knowing as just "cremation," uses high heat, typically between 1,400 and 1,800 degrees Fahrenheit, to reduce a body to bone fragments over a period of one to three hours. Those fragments are then processed into the finer consistency we recognize as ashes.

Water cremation, also called aquamation, uses a heated water and alkali solution to accomplish the same biological result through a gentler chemical process. It mimics what happens naturally in the soil when a body is buried, just compressed into a matter of hours rather than years. The body is submerged, the soft tissue dissolves, and what remains is the bone structure, which is then dried and returned to the family in the same powdery form as flame cremation ashes.

The end result looks similar. You get ashes back either way. The difference is in how you got there.

The Ashes Themselves

This is the detail that surprises most people. Water cremation returns approximately 20% more ash than flame cremation. The reason is simple: heat burns. A significant portion of bone material is lost to the combustion process in flame cremation. Water cremation doesn't involve that kind of heat loss, so a higher percentage of what was physically your dog is returned to you.

The ashes from water cremation also tend to be lighter in color, often a pale white or off-white rather than the grayish tone typical of flame cremation remains. Some families find this meaningful; others don't notice or care. It's worth knowing.

If you're planning to scatter ashes in a specific location, divide them between family members, or eventually put some into memorial jewelry, the higher volume may matter to you practically, not just emotionally.

The "Fear of Fire" Question

I'm going to name this directly because it comes up a lot, and I think it deserves a real answer rather than being talked around.

Some pet parents have a visceral discomfort with the idea of flame cremation. For some it's tied to spiritual or religious beliefs. For others it's a more instinctive reaction: this animal trusted me completely, and the idea of fire feels violent even if they know intellectually it isn't. That feeling is real and it's worth taking seriously when you're making this decision.

Water cremation was developed in part because medical schools and funeral providers recognized that many families wanted an alternative that felt less harsh. The language around it, "gentle," "natural," "like returning to the earth," isn't just marketing. It reflects something genuine about how the process works and how many families experience the choice.

If you've been carrying that discomfort and wondering whether you're being irrational, you're not. Your feelings about what happens to your dog after they die are legitimate input into this decision.

That said, many families choose flame cremation and find it a completely dignified, meaningful choice. If it doesn't bother you, it shouldn't bother you. The process is fast, it's professional, it's been the standard for decades, and providers take it seriously.

Is Pet Aquamation Worth the Extra Cost?

This is the question most people are actually trying to answer when they land here, and I want to give it a direct response.

Water cremation typically costs $100 to $200 more than flame cremation for a private service. Whether that premium is worth it depends on which factors matter most to you. If the environmental difference is meaningful to you, the extra cost buys a significant reduction in emissions and fuel use. If the 20% more ash returned matters for what you're planning to do with the remains, that's real and tangible value. If the process itself feels more aligned with how you want your dog's aftercare to go, that peace of mind has value too.

If none of those factors move the needle for you, flame cremation is a completely dignified choice and the cost difference can stay in your pocket. There is no correct answer here beyond what fits your situation.

Environmental Differences

Water cremation uses significantly less energy than flame cremation and produces no combustion byproducts. There's no smoke, no carbon from fuel burn. The liquid byproduct of the process is sterile and is typically treated as wastewater or released to a municipal system depending on local regulations.

Flame cremation does involve fuel consumption and emissions. A single pet cremation produces considerably less impact than a human cremation, but the cumulative environmental picture is real if that's a lens that matters to you.

Availability

This is the practical factor that might make the decision for you before any other consideration does.

Flame cremation is available virtually everywhere. Any veterinary clinic or pet cremation provider offers it.

Water cremation is legal in a growing number of U.S. states and Canadian provinces, but providers are still unevenly distributed. If you're in Missouri or surrounding Midwest states, availability has improved but is not yet universal — it's worth checking directly with your vet or searching "pet aquamation near me" for your specific city or region before you assume it's accessible. Some areas have multiple options; others have none within a reasonable distance.

Search tip
When you call a provider, ask specifically for aquamation or water cremation by name. Some use the terms interchangeably with services that are not actually the same thing, so ask them to describe the process so you know what you're getting.

Questions Worth Asking Any Provider

Whether you're leaning toward flame or water cremation, these questions separate a trustworthy provider from one who treats it like a transaction.

Do you offer private cremation, and what does that mean exactly? Some providers do communal cremation where multiple pets are processed together and ashes are not returned individually. If getting your specific dog's ashes back matters to you, confirm that private service is what you're paying for.

What is your actual turnaround time? The answer should be specific. "A few days" is not specific. "Three to five business days" is specific. Press for the real number.

Can I be present? Not every provider allows this, but some do, and for some families it matters enormously. Ask before you assume.

What happens to my pet between drop-off and the cremation? Transport, storage, and handling practices vary. A good provider will answer this question without hesitation.

Do you have any certifications or affiliations? The Pet Loss Professionals Alliance and the Cremation Association of North America both have member standards. Membership isn't a guarantee, but it's a reasonable signal.

So Which One Should You Choose?

The honest answer is: the one that feels right when you've thought it through, not the one that was easiest to Google at midnight.

If environmental impact is your primary driver and it's available in your area, water cremation is the clearer choice. If cost is the constraint, flame cremation is more accessible. If you have a strong feeling either way about the process itself, trust that feeling. It's valid.

What I'd encourage you to do, regardless of which direction you're leaning, is find your local provider now. Call them. Ask the questions above. Get a sense of how they treat the people who call them in their worst moments, because that tells you a lot about how they'll treat your dog.

You don't have to decide today. But knowing your options, having a name and a number saved somewhere, means that when the day comes, you're not starting from zero.
